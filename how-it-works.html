Understanding the Math

How Your Money
Actually Grows

Behind every number on our calculator is a well-known financial formula. Here's a plain-English breakdown of what's happening under the hood.

1

SIP — Systematic Investment Plan

Invest a fixed amount every month and let compound interest do the heavy lifting.

A SIP works by investing a consistent amount at regular intervals (typically monthly). Each installment earns compound interest from the date it's invested until the maturity date. The magic is that early installments compound for much longer, creating an exponential growth curve.

FV = P × [(1 + r)ⁿ − 1] / r × (1 + r)
FV Future Value — the total amount you'll have at the end
P Monthly investment amount (e.g. ₹10,000)
r Monthly interest rate = Annual Rate ÷ 12 ÷ 100
n Total number of months = Years × 12
Worked Example
  1. You invest ₹10,000/month for 20 years at 12% annual return
  2. Monthly rate: r = 12 ÷ 12 ÷ 100 = 0.01
  3. Total months: n = 20 × 12 = 240
  4. Plug into formula: FV = 10,000 × [(1.01)²⁴⁰ − 1] / 0.01 × 1.01
  5. Total invested: ₹24,00,000 (₹24 Lakh)
  6. Final value: ≈ ₹99,91,479 (nearly ₹1 Crore!)
  7. Wealth created by compounding: ≈ ₹75,91,479
2

Lumpsum — One-Time Investment

Invest a large amount once and watch it compound over time.

A lumpsum investment is the simplest form of compound interest. You put in a single amount, and it grows year after year. There are no additional contributions — just pure compounding on your initial principal.

A = P × (1 + r/n)^(n×t)
A Final amount after compounding
P Principal — the amount you invest once
r Annual interest rate (as a decimal)
n Number of times compounded per year (12 for monthly)
t Time in years
Worked Example
  1. You invest ₹5,00,000 once at 12% annual return for 15 years
  2. Monthly compounding: r/n = 0.12/12 = 0.01
  3. Total compounding periods: n×t = 12 × 15 = 180
  4. A = 5,00,000 × (1.01)¹⁸⁰
  5. Final value: ≈ ₹29,95,992
  6. Your money grew ~6x without any additional investment!
3

The Power of Compounding

Why Einstein called it the "eighth wonder of the world."

Compound interest means you earn interest on your interest. In the first year, you earn returns only on your principal. But from the second year onwards, you earn returns on your principal plus all the interest you've already earned. This creates an exponential curve.

📈

Simple Interest

You earn a fixed percentage of the original amount each year. ₹100 at 10% always earns ₹10/year. After 10 years: ₹200.

🚀

Compound Interest

You earn a percentage of the growing amount. ₹100 at 10% compounds to ₹110, then ₹121, then ₹133... After 10 years: ₹259.

Rule of 72: Years to double ≈ 72 ÷ Rate

At 12% returns, your money doubles roughly every 6 years. At 8%, it takes about 9 years.
